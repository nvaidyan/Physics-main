Theoretical physicist's collaboration leads to discovery of world's smallest possible atom

   Rich Lebed didn't wake up one morning and decide to make true muonium. In fact, it wasn't really a part of his research focus at all. But during the course of casual discussion with colleague Stanley Brodsky, Lebed and Brodsky realized that they had, in fact, just figured out how to create true muonium - a remarkably small atom first theorized more than 50 years ago. Lebed, Associate Professor of Physics at ASU, and Brodksy, Professor at the Department of Energy's SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory in Menlo Park, CA, worked together on the resulting paper - Production of the Smallest QED Atom: True Muonium - that appeared in May's Physical Review Letters. For more on Lebed's discovery - click HERE.
 

The discovery highlights the importance seminars, workshops,  conferences, and research visits play in any research effort. Bringing scientists together via these means can lead to new perspectives, expanded resources, added layers of expertise, and even serendipitous discovery. ASU Physics views such interaction as an investment in its research as well as in its students, who often have the opportunity to interface with visiting researchers and travel to conferences. ASU Physics has a range of seminars planned for 2009-10. More information coming in the August 2009 issue of Physics Flash.

ASU Physics' Bauer receives two honors in Spring 2009

Ernst Bauer, Distinguished Professor of Research in ASU Physics, was honored in Spring 2009 with two prestigious awards, adding to a long list of career achievements.
   In March, Bauer received the Humboldt Research Award during the Symposium of the Alexander von Humboldt Foundation in Bamberg, Germany. The award is granted "in recognition of a researcher's entire achievements...to academics whose fundamental discoveries , new theories, or insights have had a significant impact on their own discipline." The Humboldt Foundation recognized Bauer for his "excellent achievements in solid state physics."
   A month later, Bauer was awarded the title - Doctor honoris causa by Maria Curie-Sklodowska University in Lublin, Poland. In addition to citing his contribution to the growth of crystals and the development of the Low Energy Electron Microscope, Professor Mieczyslaw Jalochowski praised Bauer for his  thirty-year collaboration with the university, during which time he has shared "his knowledge and experience" with the community of Polish physicists.
   The honorary degree was particularly meaningful to Bauer because of the emphasis on human relationships through science. Throughout his career, he worked to establish scientific collaborations across the globe - often when others would not. Bauer's wife and fellow physicist, Anastassia Pavlovska notes that during the cold war, when scientists from eastern European countries were completely isolated and unable to perform competitive research, Ernst made a point to include scientists from those countries in his research, including seventeen scientists from Poland.
   His international collaborations extended beyond eastern Europe to include visiting scientists from the U.S., India, Japan, China, Greece, South Africa, Canada, Sweden, Korea, and Holland among others, for a total of 82 visitors. Bauer continued his collaborations after moving to ASU in 1991 with scientists from Poland, Japan, Italy, Germany, and Hong Kong. In short, his love of science, his hospitality, and generosity know no national boundaries. The impact of such relationships was showcased at the Lublin event as many current and former collaborators made the trip to honor and show their respect for their dear colleague, resulting in a truly special and heart-warming ceremony.

From the Chair...

Summer Activities

Although there are fewer students on campus during the summer, the pace of research and discovery certainly doesn’t slow down. In fact, it seems to speed up.  ASU Physics faculty, students, researchers and visitors are developing new experimental and theoretical approaches to advance our knowledge from the most fundamental concepts of matter and energy to important applications that will impact information technology, energy, and health. These are not seasonal efforts, but work that requires daily focus throughout one’s career.
   For most of us, it is evident that discoveries seldom develop from a single thought.  They build from measurements that give unanticipated results, or from an analysis or theory that changes when an additional concept is considered, or when two apparently disparate concepts are shown to impact a phenomena in an unanticipated way. 
   Physicists are prone to asking questions that they cannot answer.  In fact, a truly great question is one that no one can answer.  These ‘great questions’ can guide our thoughts, experiments and theory to follow a path that builds step by step upon itself such that an increasing number of researchers are contributing to solving the problem. Understanding dark energy and dark matter in

the universe, understanding molecular functions in a cell, using electron microscopy of solids at its ultimate resolution, finding new materials for energy applications, are just a few of the great questions that are challenging our faculty and students.
   Summer is also the time for our teacher-focused Masters of Natural Science and workshop programs to welcome middle and secondary school teachers back to our program.  In addition, for the last few years, a select group of high school teachers have been directly involved in ASU Physics research programs. In the Nanoscience Laboratory, my students are working with physics teachers Aaron Jarvis and Harrison Stratton to find new approaches for ultra low power electronics using new materials combinations in nanoscale structures.  The goal is both to advance the science and to develop learning modules that will enable public school teachers to bring first-hand experience in nanoscience to inquisitive high school students here an Arizona and beyond.
   We invite you to explore the interesting research underway in ASU Physics. I encourage you to find a way to meet our faculty and students this summer. And if you do, ask them about the ‘great question’ that is setting the path for their research.
 

 

                                                           
                                                            Robert J. Nemanich
                                                            Chair & Professor of Physics
